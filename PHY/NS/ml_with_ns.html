Type II Supernova

  • The star with a mass of \(12 \sim 40M_{\odot}\) can generate iron with Silicon-burning process.
     
  • If the mass of iron core reaches Chandrasekhar limit  (\(1.44M_{\odot}\)), electron degeneracy is no longer sufficient to counter the gravitational compression.
     
  • Iron core collapses very fast (\(0.23 c\))
    \(\Rightarrow~ T \simeq 10^{11}K\)
    \(\Rightarrow\) Electron capture
    \(\Rightarrow\) Iron becomes neutron & suddenly stop gravitational collapse by neutron degeneracy
    \(\Rightarrow\) Shockwave occurs
    \(\Rightarrow\) Supernova explosion

Neutron star 101

  • Radius ~ \(10\text{km}\)
     
  • Mass ~ \(1.3 \sim 2.2 M_{\odot}\)
     
  • Predicted at 1931, Discovered at 1967
     
  • Known 2600+
     
  • Core density ~ \(10^{15}\text{g}/\text{cm}^3\)
     
  • Spin \(\leq 716 \text{Hz}\) (42690 rpm)

Basic structure of neutron star

  • Atmosphere (\(\sim 10\text{cm}\))
    : Consist of very thin fluid/gas.
     
  • Envelope (\(\sim 100\text{m}\))
    : Consist of lattice of \(\text{Fe}\). At bottom of envelope (\(\rho \sim 10^{6}\text{g}/\text{cm}^3\)) the electrons and protons start recombining in inverse \(\beta\)-decay.
     
  • Crust (\(\sim 1\text{km}\))
    : At \(\rho \sim 3\times 10^{11}\text{g}/\text{cm}^3\), the nuclei gets unstable and the free neutrons are emitted
    - neutron drip

basic structure of neutron star

  • Outer core (\(\sim 10\text{km}\))
    : The density range \(0.5\rho_0 \leq \rho \leq 2\rho_0\). (\(\rho_0\): nuclear density)
    Contains most of the stellar mass, composed of neutrons with an admixture of protons & leptons (\(npe\mu\) matter)
    The neutrons and protons constitute a strongly interacting Fermi liquid and can become superfluid.
     
  • Inner core (\(?\))
    : Occur for only massive neutron star, compositions & properties are not well known. There are some proposed theoretical models.

Stellar structure Equations

\begin{aligned} &F_g = - \dfrac{Gm\Delta m}{r^2},~~ \Delta m = \rho(r) dr dS \\ &F_P = P(r)dS - P(r+dr)dS = - \dfrac{dP}{dr}dr dS \\ \Rightarrow~& \Delta m \ddot{r} = F_g + F_P \\ \Rightarrow~&\ddot{r} = - \dfrac{Gm}{r^2} - \left(\dfrac{1}{\rho}\right)\dfrac{\partial P}{\partial r} \end{aligned}

describe neutron star

  • Neutron star is one of the densest stellar object.
    \(\Rightarrow\) To describe NS, we need General relativity.
    \(\Rightarrow\) Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equation.
     
  • NS is very cold object. (After 1 month, \(T \sim \text{keV}\) while \(\mu \simeq E_F \sim \text{GeV} \))
    \(\Rightarrow\) To describe inner structure of NS, we need Low temperature Nuclear physics.
    \(\Rightarrow\) Equation of State.
     
  • But in NS, relative change in the metric over the average distance of baryons is exceedingly small.
    \(\Rightarrow\) To describe interaction in NS, we need Quantum Chromodynamics.
    \(\Rightarrow\) Walecka model

setup geometry

For static, non-rotating and spherical symmetric star, we can write metric as follows

ds^2 = -e^{2\Phi(r)}dt^2 + e^{2\Lambda(r)}dr^2 + r^2 d\Omega^2

Consider perfect fluid matter.
From energy-momentum conservation (\(\nabla_\nu T^{\mu\nu} = 0 \)), we can obtain next equation.

\dfrac{dP}{dr} = - (\rho + P) \dfrac{d\Phi}{dr}

From Einstein equation for (\(r \leq R,\, G_{00} = 8\pi T_{00}\)),

From Einstein equation for (\(r \leq R,\, G_{11} = 8\pi T_{11}\)),

\dfrac{d}{dr} \left[r(1 - e^{-2\Lambda}) \right] = 8\pi r^2 \rho(r) = 2 \dfrac{dm(r)}{dr}
\displaystyle \therefore e^{-2\Lambda} = 1 - \frac{2m(r)}{r}
\displaystyle \frac{d\Phi}{dr} = \frac{m(r) + 4\pi r^3 P}{r^2(1 - \frac{2m(r)}{r})}

Tolman-oppenheimer-volkoff equation

\begin{aligned} \dfrac{dm}{dr} &= 4\pi r^2 \rho \\ \dfrac{dP}{dr} &= - \dfrac{\rho m}{r^2} \left( 1 + \dfrac{P}{\rho}\right) \left(1 + \dfrac{4\pi P r^3}{m}\right) \left(1 - \frac{2m}{r} \right)^{-1} \end{aligned}

Combine all equations of previous slides, then we can get next equations.

These equation is called Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equation. (TOV equation)

Newtonian VS General Relativity

\begin{aligned} \dfrac{dm}{dr} &= 4\pi r^2 \rho \\ \dfrac{dP}{dr} &= - \dfrac{\rho m}{r^2} \left( 1 + \dfrac{P}{\rho}\right) \left(1 + \dfrac{4\pi P r^3}{m}\right) \left(1 - \frac{2m}{r} \right)^{-1} \end{aligned}
\begin{aligned} \dfrac{dm}{dr} &= 4\pi r^2 \rho \\ \dfrac{dP}{dr} &= - \dfrac{\rho m}{r^2} \end{aligned}

Data generation - 1.EoS generation

  • Divide \(\rho\) interval \([\rho_0, 8\rho_0]\)  into 5 pieces
     
  • Randomly (but causally) choose average sound velocity in each segment
    : \(\epsilon < c^2_{s,i} < 1-\epsilon~\text{where}~ c=1, ~\epsilon = 0.01\)
     
  • Calculate pressure node points : \(p_i = p_{i-1} + c^2_{s,i} (\rho_i - \rho_{i-1})\)
     
  • Use SLy4 for \(\rho \leq \rho_0\) region \(\Rightarrow~p_0 = p(\rho_0)\)
     
  • For \(\rho > \rho_0\), interpolate by piecewise polytrope function.
    : \(P(\rho) = K_i \rho^{\Gamma_i}\) for \(\rho_{i-1} < \rho < \rho_i\)
